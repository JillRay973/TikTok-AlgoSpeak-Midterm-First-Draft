I. The Setup
censored

Someone around 2021 decided the word "suicide" was dangerous.

Or at least, that's what millions of TikTok creators came to believe. And so, over a period of a few years in the early 2020s, a new vocabulary quietly emerged from the platform. "Unalived" replaced suicide or death. "Seggs" stood in for sex. "Grape" became rape. "Corn" became porn. These are not typos. They started as jokes, many people using them ironically, but they became something more ambient and strange than that. They became, for many users, the only way they knew how to talk.

I've been using this language myself. Not in conversation, necissarily, but definitely online. My brain has quietly absorbed the substitutions. And when I started looking at the research for this project, I kept bumping into a question I couldn't shake: Who actually told us to talk this way?

"Are TikTok creators using euphemisms to evade algorithmic censorship, or have they adopted comfort language that's become self-sustaining? And does the distinction even matter when it's reshaping how millions talk?"

I don't think TikTok ever explicitly told anyone to say "unalived." Not in any direct, published, findable way. And I think that might be the more disturbing story.

II. The Words

A short glossary of things we said instead

suicide / death →
"Unalived"

Went mainstream across mental health content. Now appears in student essays, museum placards, and everyday speech well outside TikTok.

sex →
"Seggs"

A phonetic respelling that became an ironic in-joke. TikTok's moderation eventually caught on, forcing further mutation into "s3x," then new terms entirely.

rape →
"Grape"

Used in sexual assault awareness content. A deadly serious topic forced to wear an absurd costume to survive the algorithm.

pornography →
"Corn 🌽"

An emoji chain: porn → corn → 🌽. Each abstraction layer adds distance. The absurdity is the point, and also the cost.

lesbian →
"Le dollar bean"

A playful misread that became community shorthand, and a signal that LGBTQ+ content was being disproportionately caught by automated systems.

ICE raids / immigration protests →
"Cute winter boots"

The newest evolution: entire political topics hidden behind lifestyle hashtags. Resistance vocabulary with no dictionary, invented in real time.

Linguist Kendra Calhoun and Alexia Fawcett called this "linguistic innovation as textual censorship avoidance." Adam Aleksic, whose 2025 book Algospeak is the most comprehensive treatment of the phenomenon, argues this constitutes a genuine dialect, language that evolves not from community expression, but from constraint. Researcher Nicole Holliday at UC Berkeley studies how this kind of slang doesn't stay put. It migrates, changes meaning, becomes something else entirely.

The thing I notice about this glossary: these words weren't invented because they're more precise or expressive. They were invented to trick a machine. And now they're just… words.

By the Numbers

The hashtag #unalive has accumulated over 600 million views on TikTok, a word that didn't exist a decade ago, now more searched than the clinical term it replaced.

TikTok took down over 106 million videos in a single quarter for guideline violations, 80% within 24 hours of posting. That's the environment creators are navigating.

~⅓
of U.S. social media users

A 2022 poll found nearly one-third of American social media users reported using "emojis or alternative phrases" to avoid content moderation, before algospeak was even widely named.

0

TikTok has never published an explicit list of banned or suppressed terms. Users are self-policing against a phantom, and the platform has never corrected that misunderstanding.

On Uneven Enforcement

Research consistently shows that algospeak-driven suppression doesn't fall evenly. Smaller creators, Black creators, and disabled creators report significantly higher rates of shadow-banning than their larger counterparts. The algorithm doesn't just suppress topics, it suppresses people unevenly, further perpetuating inequalities online as well as in real life.

"The answer is that no one really knows. It's extremely unclear whether there actually is censorship, and to what degree. TikTok is super cagey about sharing this stuff." — Linguist Eleanor Stern, via Tortoise Media
IV. The Policy Gap

TikTok does not publish a banned words list.

I read the Community Guidelines and Policices. All of it. And ctrl + f'd it for good measure. The TikTok Community Guidelines exist, and they're vague in the way all platform policies are vague, broad prohibitions on harmful content, without specific language. There is no document that says "do not say the word suicide." There is no list of forbidden terms handed down from ByteDance.

What there is instead is a black box. An algorithm that may or may not deprioritize certain content, with no appeals process, no feedback loop, and no official acknowledgement of which words it's reacting to. Users believed they experienced reall suppression and responded by reverse-engineering what they thought the system wanted.

Researchers who interviewed 19 TikTok creators found that participants "largely anticipated how TikTok's algorithm would read their videos, and used algospeak to evade unjustified content moderation." They were anticipating a system they'd never seen documented. They were writing rules for a game that never published its rulebook.

Key Finding

The gap between what users believe is censored and what is documented policy is enormous. That gap is not accidental. Opacity benefits platforms. It makes users cautious, compliant, self-policing. It's cheaper than actual moderation, and produces a population that enforces the platform's preferences on itself.

The Paradox

If the algorithm suppresses content about suicide and assault, using softer language doesn't make those topics safer, it makes them harder to discuss clearly. Harm reduction conversations, crisis resources, survivor stories are all obscured behind a vocabulary nobody voted for.

V. Censored or Self-Inflicted?
Both.

And that's worse.

This is where the research gets uncomfortable, because I think the easy take, that "TikTok is censoring important speech" is incomplete in a way that lets us off the hook and fails to hold us accountable for our own part in it.

Yes, TikTok's content moderation has real, documented issues. LGBTQ+ content has been shadowbanned. Mental health creators have reported suppression. These are real things. But even if every instance of suppression were lifted tomorrow, I suspect the language would stay. It's become trendy. It's become a signal that you're in on something.

We've started self-censoring not out of fear, but out of habit. And there's something about that which feels more insidious than the algorithm itself.

When platforms obscure the mechanics of suppression, users can't fight what they can't identify. They just adapt. And over time, adaptation starts to look like choice.

This is the same dynamic I found while researching Apple Music's algorithm earlier this semester. The algorithm doesn't announce its biases. It just keeps serving you the same twenty songs until you forget there were others. The narrowing feels, from the inside, like preference. On TikTok, the narrowing is linguistic. The algorithm may have kicked it off, but we've taken over. We're doing it to ourselves now, and we're calling it slang.

Nick Palffy draws a direct line between euphemistic algospeak and Orwellian doublespeak, language that softens, obscures, and makes serious things harder to name. Suicide becomes "unalived." Rape becomes "grape." The softening isn't neutral. It says that this topic is too dangerous for plain language, and once we accept that, we've ceded something real.

VI. Leaks into Reality

The words don't stay on TikTok.

The Kurt Cobain museum placard is the example that sits with me most. An actual institution, a place of historical memory, used the word "unalived" to describe his death by suicide, sparking genuine public debate about whether that language was appropriate for a historical record. Educator Will Stevenson has documented algospeak appearing in student essays. Researchers track its spread into everyday conversation, marketing copy, and now journalism.

This is how platform logic colonizes reality. It doesn't stay behind the screen. The mental shortcuts and social contracts that emerge from algorithmic pressure become so natural that we export them without thinking. This is, in miniature, what every major platform does to language and culture, training us to communicate in ways that are platform-friendly, then watching that training walk out the door with us.

The Bigger Picture

What TikTok algospeak illustrates is that platforms don't need to explicitly ban speech to change how we speak. They just need to create an environment of uncertainty with opaque rules, inconsistent enforcement, adaptation rewarded. The result is a population that has internalized the platform's preferences and begun enforcing them on itself. This is, if anything, more efficient than censorship.

VII. My "Hot Take"

I don't think the answer is to just say the "bad" words back.

I want to be careful not to sound like someone who thinks the solution is simply being more provocative. Language evolves. Communities develop their own vocabularies. There is something kind, even, in the desire to make difficult conversations more approachable, to lower the stakes for someone who needs to talk.

But I do think we should know where our words come from, and I think we should be honest that a significant portion of TikTok algospeak didn't emerge organically from the communities that use it, it emerged from fear. Fear of a machine that nobody fully understands, operating according to rules nobody published.

The research points to a real gap between what TikTok's policies actually prohibit and what users believe they prohibit. That gap is not an accident, and it produces, as a byproduct, a generation of people who have learned to soften their speech in ways that primarily serve the algorithm's comfort, not theirs.

Language shapes reality. If we've outsourced the shaping to a black box, we should at least know that we've done it.

I'm writing this essay on a website, which feels appropriately ironic. I also used the word "suicide" several times in this piece. And while it felt briefly daring, I also then felt sad that it felt daring. That's the thing about internalized algorithmic logic. You notice it most in the moments you push back against it, and feel the resistance in yourself first. The platform doesn't have to do anything. You've already done it for them.
